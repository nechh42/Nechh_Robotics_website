Position Sizing: The Only Way to Survive Long-Term

March 2026 · 7 min read · By Nechh Robotics Research Team

Two traders use the same strategy. One survives a 10-trade losing streak and goes on to profit. The other blows up their account. The difference? Position sizing. It's not glamorous, but it is the single most important variable in determining whether you stay in the game long enough for edge to manifest.

The Three Main Approaches

1. Fixed Dollar Amount

Risk the same dollar amount per trade (e.g., always risk $100). Simple, but doesn't account for account growth or volatility changes.

  • Pro: Easy to implement, predictable risk in dollar terms
  • Con: As account grows, risk becomes a smaller percentage — limiting compounding

2. Fixed Fractional (% of Capital)

Risk a fixed percentage of current capital per trade (e.g., always risk 1% of account). This is the standard approach for systematic trading.

  • Pro: Position size grows with account (compounding), shrinks during drawdowns (protection)
  • Con: Requires discipline — 1% of $10,000 feels small but 1% of $100,000 is $1,000

3. Kelly Criterion

A formula that calculates the mathematically optimal bet size given your edge: Kelly % = W − (1 − W) / R, where W = win rate and R = win/loss ratio.

  • Pro: Theoretically maximizes long-term growth rate
  • Con: Full Kelly is too aggressive in practice — most professional traders use half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly

4. ATR-Based Sizing (Our Approach)

Risk a fixed percentage of capital, but adjust position size based on current market volatility (ATR). In high-volatility conditions, trade smaller. In low-volatility conditions, trade larger.

ATR Position Size Formula Position Size = (Account × Risk%) ÷ (ATR × Multiplier)
Example: ($10,000 × 1%) ÷ ($200 ATR × 1.0) = 0.5 units

How We Size Positions at Nechh Robotics

  1. Base risk per signal: 1% of subscriber-suggested capital (we signal, subscriber executes)
  2. ATR multiplier: Stop-loss is always placed at 1.0× ATR below entry
  3. Max concurrent positions: 3 (prevents over-concentration in correlated moves)
  4. Drawdown halt: If 3 consecutive losses, signal frequency reduces by 50% for 48 hours

The Psychological Component

Position sizing is also a psychological tool. Trading too large relative to your account causes emotional decision-making — cutting winners early, letting losers run, and abandoning the strategy at the worst possible moment (the trough of a drawdown, right before recovery).

A position size that lets you sleep at night is, objectively, the right position size. The mathematically optimal size is useless if you can't execute it emotionally.

Rule of Thumb If you're thinking about a trade more than twice a day, you're sized too large. Reduce size until each trade feels boring. Boring is sustainable. Excitement is a risk indicator.

Conclusion

The best strategy in the world loses to the wrong position sizing. Conversely, a mediocre strategy with excellent position sizing can survive long enough to find and fix its edge. Start conservative, prove the strategy works in live conditions, then scale.

We recommend starting any new signal service or strategy at 0.5%–1% risk per trade until you have 30+ live trades. Only then consider increasing to 1.5%–2%.
